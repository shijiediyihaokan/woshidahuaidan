## The Short Answer

Worm gearboxes can be self-locking, but it's not guaranteed. The self-locking ability depends primarily on the **lead angle** of the worm and the **coefficient of friction** between the worm and gear materials.

## When Is a Worm Gearbox Self-Locking?

A worm gearbox is considered **statically self-locking** when the worm cannot be driven backwards by the gear. This occurs when:

- The lead angle is less than the friction angle
- Typically, lead angles below 5° provide reliable self-locking
- The gear ratio is high (usually above 30:1)

## Factors That Affect Self-Locking

1. **Lead Angle**: Smaller angles = more self-locking
2. **Material Pair**: Bronze gear + hardened steel worm = better friction
3. **Lubrication**: Less lubrication = more friction = more self-locking (but more wear)
4. **Vibration**: External vibration can break the self-locking condition
5. **Temperature**: Changes material properties and lubrication viscosity

## Important Safety Note

**Never rely on worm gearbox self-locking as a safety brake.** Always use a dedicated braking system for lifting applications where failure could cause injury.
